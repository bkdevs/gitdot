I hate describing what I do.

The word founder has a perfume to it that I’d rather not wear. An engineer is apt, a title I should take more pride in, but is admittedly plain. I’ve used the word builder before, but that too is seeming a bit too Ayn Randian nowadays.

But when asked to guess my occupation, the phrase that I hear the most is “UX designer.” (undoubtedly a consequence of my clothes, the black balloon pants and rimmed glasses).

But as much as I find that funny, it is seemingly becoming a reality. \
I am spending more and more of my days staring at my monitor and ruminating pixels.

Design is something we intend to differentiate on. \
I want gitdot to be well-designed and I want that to be evident in each interaction that the user takes. That is both a want (I find it hard not to) and a need.

&nbsp;

Design is important. \
How a product looks and how a product feels leaves an impression — and that impression itself can be a differentiator.

Linear is an example we look up to. \
There are many project management tools, and if you were to value them all solely based off what they can do, the whole market you should consider a commodity.

But they won by sheer design. \
People _like_ using Linear.

That’s sometimes easy to forget in tech, where we tend to fixate on the cause-and-effect: the logical reason as to why a product is possible now that it wasn’t before.

But that isn’t the whole story, for tech and certainly not for other industries. \
The reason I purchase clothes from the brands that I do isn’t because that brand is the _only_ or the _first_ to make that item, it’s because I like the product. I like the design, I like the quality, and I like the care that was put into it.

So although design can feel frivolous at times, I am committed.

I want people to like using gitdot. \
And to that end, I’ll spare no expense.

&nbsp;

About three months ago, I wrote down the following tenets:
1. Form follows function — products are meant to be used.
2. Remove what doesn't add — make every pixel count.
3. Less is more — do few things and do them well.

And in each feature I’ve designed since, I’ve attempted to adhere to the above.

What I’ve learned is that is hard to do so. \
It is easy to 1) design without thinking and 2) to get attached to your proclivity.

As an example, I was very adamantly against file trees at the start. \
I thought they generally just looked ugly and was a component that was simply hard to make look right. And furthermore, I rarely use a file tree in my own development. I’m a vim guy who just always fuzzy-finds his way to success.

And lastly, I felt strongly that gitdot should _not_ look like an IDE. \
I hated when GitHub was trying to push Codespaces and thought that a browser-based IDE would always be fundamentally inferior to a native version.

My belief is that good products are contextual: they understand the context in which they are used in and do the functionality that is apt for them. Most developers _will_ have an IDE open alongside their browser, so why try and replicate the same? Should we not focus on what we can uniquely do instead?

That tantrum compelled me to adopt a telescoping file finder (e.g., like ranger) in the initial draft of the UI, which looked a bit like this.

&nbsp;

This week, I made a file tree. \
And what was a bit surprising is that it was incredibly usable.

There’s a few times where the interplay between technology and design is made apparent and this was one of them. By making our pages load really really fast, gitdot does kind of work as an IDE, and it feels like a good one.

I still think it is right that we optimize for reads (instead of do a bad job at writes), but I do think it now possible that a website can be a best-in-class experience for browsing code.

This required quite a lot of iteration to get to the state that you see above.

An overall focus was the experience of _browsing code._ \
That is, you are new to this repository and want to visualize folder structure and click around to get a feel for how things work, as opposed to the fuzzy-file use case where you already know what it is that you want.

That was something that also took some debate to reconcile. \
Part of me, and especially initially, was adamant about building for the maintainer, not the average user.

But I’ve also realized that 1) the most common user of a web-based code browser is somebody that is _not_ working on the repository and that 2) this is a tool for the maintainer to present himself.

A lot of people take a lot of pride in their code, and if you’re the type to obsess over your folder structure and interfaces, wouldn’t you want to show it? Wouldn’t you want a tool that helps you show it? And that, I think, is something unique to the role that we can play.

&nbsp;

To end on, it’s been a little unclear what a moniker for gitdot’s design has been thus far. \
Minimalism is a word, and an absence of is a mantra that I live by.

But it’s become quite clear recently what it is — it’s a CLI. \
gitdot is a CLI-inspired website.

What’s unique about CLIs is that they take action with a single key. \
They transform your keyboard into a new physical device entirely, as opposed to the rather lethargic mode of interaction in which users click on a button, type something in, and then take action.

That feels quite powerful indeed.
